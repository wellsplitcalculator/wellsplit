About this calculator

Many couples merge finances for shared goals but still want personal spending money that doesn't require permission or justification. This tool takes your combined income, deducts shared obligations and savings, then splits the remaining pool into individual allowances. You can choose an income‑proportional split (each contributes to shared costs according to earning power) or a 50/50 split. A built‑in buffer helps leave room for unexpected expenses.

How to use the spending allowance calculator

Follow these simple steps to find each partner's fair personal spending allowance:

  1. Enter both incomes — Add Person A and Person B's monthly take-home pay. If only one person earns, enter zero for the other.
  2. Add shared monthly expenses — Include rent, utilities, groceries, insurance, and any other recurring household costs you both share.
  3. Set a joint savings target — Decide how much you want to save together each month for emergencies, vacations, or future goals.
  4. Choose an expense split method — Pick "Proportional to income" if you want each person to contribute based on what they earn, or "50/50 equal split" for a straight down-the-middle approach.
  5. Select a buffer percentage — A small cushion (5–15%) helps cover surprise costs without derailing your allowances.
  6. Hit Calculate — The tool shows each person's allowance, the buffer amount, and a full breakdown of the math. Copy the result to share or save it.

The allowance is money each person can spend on hobbies, clothes, gadgets, gifts, or anything personal — no questions asked.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a personal spending allowance for couples?

It's an agreed monthly amount each partner can spend on hobbies, clothes, gadgets, gifts, or anything personal — without needing to check with the other person. It sits outside shared bills and joint savings.

Should allowances be equal or based on income?

There's no single right answer. Proportional-to-income splits can feel fair when earnings differ significantly, because both partners contribute to shared costs at a similar relative rate. Equal allowances work well when partners view all income as fully shared. The calculator lets you compare both methods.

What does the buffer percentage do?

The buffer sets aside a percentage of the leftover money (after bills and savings) as a household cushion. It reduces the individual allowances slightly but helps cover surprise expenses, keeping the budget realistic.

How is the allowance amount calculated?

First we subtract shared expenses and joint savings from total household income. Then we apply your chosen buffer percentage to the remaining amount. What's left is split into two personal allowances using your selected method (income‑based or 50/50).

What counts as a shared expense?

Rent or mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance, car payments, childcare, streaming services you both use, and any other recurring household costs. Personal items like individual phone plans or solo hobbies are not shared expenses — those come from your allowance.

Can we use this calculator if only one person earns income?

Absolutely. Enter the earned income for one partner and zero for the other. A proportional split will assign the full shared-cost burden to the earning partner, while an equal split treats both as contributing equally from the pooled household income. The tool works either way.

Is this the same as the 50/30/20 budget rule?

It follows a similar philosophy but is tailored for couples. The 50/30/20 rule says 50% for needs, 30% for wants, 20% for savings. This calculator focuses specifically on the "wants" portion and divides it fairly between two people after covering shared needs and savings.

How often should we recalculate our allowances?

A good habit is to revisit whenever income changes, a new shared expense appears, or savings goals shift. Many couples check in quarterly or after major life events like a raise, move, or new baby.
